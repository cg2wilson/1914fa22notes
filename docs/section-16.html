Section 1.6 Calculating Limits Using Limit Laws

Objectives

  • State limit laws and use the laws (if appropriate) to determine the limit of a function using multiple representations

  • Use the Direct Substitution Property to evaluate limits algebraically

  • Develop strategies to compute \(\dfrac{0}{0}\) indeterminate forms

  • Apply the Limit Inequality Theorem and Squeeze Theorem to determine limits

Subsection 1.6.1 Before Class

Subsubsection 1.6.1.1 Limit Laws

Theorem 1.6.1. Limit Laws.
Suppose that \(c\) is a constant, and that the limits \(\ds \lim_{x\to a} f(x)\) and \(\ds \lim_{x\to a}\) exist. Then,

  1. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to a} [f(x) \pm g(x)] = \ds \lim_{x\to a} f(x) \pm \lim_{x\to a} g(x)\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to a} [cf(x)] = c\cdot \lim_{x\to a} f(x)\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to a} [f(x)\cdot g(x)] = \lim_{x\to a} f(x)\cdot \lim_{x\to a} g(x)\)

  4. \(\ds \lim_{x\to a} \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \dfrac{\ds \lim_{x\to a} f(x)}{\ds \lim_{x\to a} g(x)}\text{,}\) provided that \(\ds \lim_{x\to a} g(x)\neq 0\)

  5. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to a} [f(x)]^n = \left[\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\right]^n\)

  6. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to a} c = c\)

  7. \(\ds \lim_{x\to a} [f(x)]^{1/n} = \left[\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\right]^{1/n}\text{,}\) where \(n\) is a positive integer (we assume the limit is positive if \(n\) is even)

  1. Sum/Difference Rule: The limit of a sum/difference of functions is the sum/difference of the limit of the functions.

  2. Constant Multiplier Rule: The limit of a constant times a function is the constant times the limit of the function.

  3. Product Rule: The limit of a product of functions is the product of the limits.

  4. Quotient Rule: The limit of a quotient of functions is the quotient of the limits (provided the denominator is nonzero).

  5. Power Rule: The limit of a function raised to a power is the power of the limit of the function.

  6. Constant Rule: The limit of a constant is a constant.

  7. Root Rule: The limit of the \(n\)th-root of a function is the \(n\)th-root of the limit of a function.

Example 1.6.2.
Use the limit laws to determine the following limits, if they exist. The function \(f(x)\) is graphed as the solid line, and \(g(x)\) is graphed as the dashed line.

  1. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to 1} [f(x)]\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to -1} [g(x)]\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to -2}\left[\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right]\)

  4. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to -1}\left[f(x)g(x)\right]\)

  5. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to -1}\left[-2f(x)+3g(x)\right]\)

  6. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to 2} [3f(x)]\)

Direct Substitution Property.

If \(f\) is a polynomial, rational, or algebraic function with \(a\) in the domain of \(f\text{,}\) then \(\ds \lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \ds \lim_{x\to 1} \dfrac{x-1}{x^2+1}\)

Subsubsection 1.6.3.1 Two Theorems

Theorem 1.6.11. Limit Inequality.
If \(f(x)\leq g(x)\) when \(x\) is near \(a\) (except possibly at \(a\)) and the limits of \(f\) and \(g\) both exist as \(x\) approaches \(a\text{,}\) then \(\ds\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\leq \lim_{x\to a} g(x)\)
Theorem 1.6.12. Squeeze Theorem.
If \(f(x)\leq g(x)\leq h(x)\) when \(x\) is near \(a\) (except possibly at \(a\)) and \(\ds\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = \ds\lim_{x\to a} h(x) = L\text{,}\) then \(\ds \lim_{x\to a} g(x) = L\)
