Section 5.3 Volumes by Cylindrical Shells

Objectives

  • Determine whether the Shell Method, Disk Method, or Washer Method is most appropriate to compute the volume of a solid of revolution, and compute the volume by integrating with respect to \(x\) or \(y\)

Subsection 5.3.1 Before Class

Subsubsection 5.3.1.1 Slicing with Cylinders

Example 5.3.1.
Let \(f(x) = x-x^2\text{.}\)

  1. Sketch the region bounded by \(f(x)\) and the \(x-\)axis.

  2. Set up (but do not solve) an integral to find the volume of the solid created by rotating the region about the line \(y = 0\text{.}\) Why can we use the disk method?

  3. Are there problems if we instead rotate about the line \(x = 0\text{?}\) Explain with pictures, words, etc.

Example 5.3.2.
Again consider \(f(x) = x-x^2\text{.}\)

  1. Draw four midpoint rectangles for the area of the region bounded by \(f(x)\) and the \(x-\)axis. Be sure to label things appropriately!

  2. Sketch what happens to the rectangles when rotated about the line \(x = 0\text{.}\)

  3. How can we find the surface area of the shape from part (b)?

  4. Use your answers from parts (b) and (c) to approximate the volume of the solid.

  5. Write an expression that uses “infinitely many” rectangles to approximate the the volume of the solid.

  6. Convert your answer from part (d) into an integral expression, and evaluate it.

Subsubsection 5.3.1.2 The Shell Method

Method of Cylindrical Shells.

Let \(f(x)\) be a continuous function on the interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then, the volume of the solid created by rotating the region bounded by \(f(x)\) and the \(x-\)axis about the line \(x = 0\) is given by

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b 2\pi r(x)f(x)\, dx \end{equation*}

where \(r(x)\) is the radius function.

Example 5.3.3.
Consider the region bounded by the curve \(y = 2\sqrt{x}\text{,}\) the \(x-\)axis, and the line \(x = 4\text{.}\)

  1. Sketch and label the region.

  2. Sketch and label a typical approximating cylinder, when rotated about \(x = 0\text{.}\)

  3. Use the formula for the cylinder to set up an integral for the volume of the resulting solid, then evaluate it.

Example 5.3.8.
